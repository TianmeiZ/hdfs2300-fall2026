Course Description

This course examines the interaction processes of family systems including communication processes, bonding behaviors, management of conflict and aggression, and negotiation of family crises. We will be applying three perspectives (structural, intergenerational, and contextual models) to our exploration of family functioning.

Course Goals and Objectives

By the end of the semester, students should be able to:

  • Analyze different definitions and the sources of these definitions of “family”
  • Apply family systems perspectives to family dynamics and interaction processes, both within and across generations
  • Analyze the role of power in family systems.
  • Identify communication and conflict strategies in family relationships and the outcomes of various strategies.
  • Explain how contextual factors influence families and their abilities to function.
  • Critique theories, policies, and popular representations of families.
  • Understand the strengths of unique family cultures and diverse family forms and structures.

Reading Quizzes (150 points total)

Before we start covering each chapter in class, you will take a multiple-choice reading quiz to make sure that you keep up with the readings and are prepared to learn about and discuss the material in class. Quizzes are posted on HuskyCT and are due by the start of class time (2:30 p.m.) on the day we are scheduled to begin a chapter in class. Please note, they are NOT due on any particular day of the week! Students often start noticing a pattern and then think “oh, they are always due on x day,” but they are not – they are due on whatever day we start a chapter in class! It is a good idea to take the quiz early, so that technical issues do not interfere with your ability to complete the quiz, and actually, you can take quizzes any time before the due date. Therefore, I do not accept late quizzes even if it is not “your fault,” and there are no make-up quizzes unless there are emergency circumstances that result in you missing more than one week of class (e.g., hospitalized for more than a week, with verified documentation provided).

However, there will be 17 quizzes (one per chapter + a syllabus quiz), each with 10 multiple choice questions, worth 10 points each for a total of 170 possible points, but the grading of quizzes will be “out of” 150 points; that is to say, only your top 15 of 17 quiz scores count (150 points total); your two lowest scores are automatically dropped, which covers missed or low-scoring quizzes without needing make-ups.

You will have one attempt at each quiz and they are open book, but quizzes are timed – you have 15 minutes to take the quiz, so do not count on having time to look up the answers. Make sure you do the reading first.

Assignments (140 points total)

For most chapters, we will have journal entry activities in the end that ask you to reflect on how the specific topics covered in class relate to your own life, media, or news you’ve seen. These activities will be given in class and will always relate to the topics being discussed that day. They will be due before the beginning of the next class (the class we move to the next new chapter).

The Magic Word

For each activity, I will display a “magic random word” on the screen. When you submit your journal entry, you must include that word on the first line of your submission. Failing to include the word indicates that you were not present when the assignment was given, and you will receive a 0 for that assignment. This policy also applies if you arrive late or leave class early and you didn’t see the word. Note: The journal entry tied to the Two American Families documentary (the week of November 18) does not require the magic word, since there is no in-class session that week; submission instructions for that entry will be posted on HuskyCT.

There will be a total of 16 journal entry assignments, each worth 10 points. Two of the lowest scores will be dropped; thus 14 entries will count toward your final grade for a total of 140 points. For this reason, students need not e-mail me if an assignment will be missed for sickness, etc.

In extraordinary circumstances which would prevent you from completing assignments for a longer period of time, please email me with documentation of an excusable reason (e.g., hospitalization) as soon as possible, and you can make up unlimited (within reason) assignments during that designated time window. The following criteria need to be satisfied:

  • Provide documentation upon your return to class (or before, if possible).
  • Complete the assignments within one week of your return to class. Otherwise the assignments will not be accepted.

Exams (350 points)

There will be three exams in this class: a mini-exam on the foundational material (50 points), a midterm (100 points), and a cumulative final exam (200 points). All exams will be held online on HuskyCT and consist of multiple-choice questions. You will take each exam on your own — we will not meet as a class on the scheduled exam days — and each exam will be available to open during a 48-hour window (between 12:01 a.m. the first day and 11:59 p.m. the second day, ET, on the scheduled exam dates). The time allotted for each exam varies depending on the number of questions on that particular exam. The final exam will be 2 hours. The exams are open-book and open-note; however, please do not assume that having your slides and notes on hand means you don't need to prepare. The time limit is deliberately set so that you will not have enough time to look up every answer from scratch — you should study and review the material beforehand, just as you would for any exam.

You will be responsible for and tested on information presented during lectures, class discussions, media presentations, and your assigned readings. You must complete each exam entirely on your own, without assistance from others. Because each exam remains open for a 48-hour window, you may not discuss, share, or post any exam questions, answers, or content with anyone — including classmates who have not yet taken the exam — until the window has closed for the entire class. Doing so is a violation of academic integrity. If you have accommodations for exams through the Center for Students with Disabilities, please contact me at the beginning of the semester to make arrangements. You must request it – it is not set up for you automatically.

Exams will not be administered early, and make-up exams will only be given in two situations, which have different notice requirements:

AI Policy

Generative AI can be a valuable tool that facilitates learning in many ways. It is a resource that the new generation should actively learn to use in order to keep up with technological developments. However, AI must be used appropriately and ethically. Students should never provide AI tools with identifying information, confidential information, or sensitive personal information. Based on the design of this course, the use of AI tools is restricted as follows:

Quizzes and Exams: Do not use AI. The use of AI tools is strictly prohibited during quizzes and exams. These assessments are designed to evaluate your individual understanding of course concepts and your ability to apply what you have learned. Using AI during a quiz or exam constitutes a violation of this course policy and may result in penalties under the University's academic integrity procedures, including, but not limited to, receiving a score of zero on the assessment and/or additional consequences depending on the circumstances and severity of the violation.

Written Assignments: AI tools may be used as a learning aid for written assignments (e.g., brainstorming, improving grammar, or organizing ideas), but the final submission must reflect your own understanding, analysis, and writing. Any use of AI must be appropriately acknowledged at the end of your assignment. For example, you might write, "I used AI for proofreading" or "I used AI to gather background information from [website name]." Failure to disclose AI use will be considered a violation of this course policy and may result in a zero on the assignment.

Learning and Understanding Class Content: It is always recommended that you consult with me first, as AI has the potential to mislead users due to hallucinations. In other words, AI may defer to your assumptions or provide inaccurate conclusions rather than correct information. If you choose to use AI for tutoring purposes, you are responsible for verifying the accuracy of the information and for any mistakes or misunderstandings that result from relying on AI-generated content.

Again, this class will not penalize you for using AI appropriately; but it will penalize you for using AI in prohibited situations or for failing to properly acknowledge its use when disclosure is required. I recognize that no AI detection tool provides definitive evidence of AI misuse. However, if there are concerns regarding the originality of your work, especially supported by multiple detection tools consistently, you may be asked to explain your thinking process and the development of your work.

University Policies

Academic Integrity

The University of Connecticut is an institution that is dedicated to learning. Integrity, honesty, and fairness are the foundation of the educational process. Academic integrity is the pursuit of scholarly activity free from fraud and deception and is an educational objective of this institution. Academic misconduct violates these principles, and demeans not only the student committing the act of misconduct, but the entire University community. Violating academic integrity is considered a serious offense by both the university and me and is treated accordingly. Violations of academic integrity can be punishable by failure on the specific assignment or portion of the course, failure of the entire course, or other sanctions as imposed by the university. Academic misconduct is defined in Responsibilities of Community Life: The Student Code, Part VI, Section A, p. 17:

Academic misconduct includes, but is not limited to, providing or receiving assistance in a manner not authorized by the instructor in the creation of work to be submitted for academic evaluation (e.g., papers, projects, and examinations); any attempt to influence improperly (e.g., bribery, threats) any member of the faculty, staff, or administration of the University in any matter pertaining to academics or research; presenting, as one’s own, the ideas or words of another for academic evaluation; doing unauthorized work for which another person will receive credit to be evaluated; and presenting the same or substantially the same papers or projects in two or more courses without the explicit permission of the instructors involved.

A student who knowingly assists another student in committing an act of academic misconduct shall be equally accountable for the violation and shall be subject to the sanctions and other remedies described in the Student Code.
